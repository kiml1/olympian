Olympians

What makes an Olympian? Are athletes becoming more powerful each year? Why athletes from a handful number of countries perform better than others?

The Olympics can be considered one of the most popular, prestigious and competitive sporting events in the world. Its symbol represents the union of the five continents of the world and the meeting of athletes from throughout the world at the Olympic Games. In midst of the global pandemic of Coronavirus in 2020, for the first time, the Olympic Games Tokyo 2020 are posponed. The impacts of the postponement of the Olympic Game s are really hard to measure especially for the athletes who train for most of their lives to this event.

Despite having the idea of equality and fairness, getting an Olympic medal is not always just about talent. There are more than 70 countries that never won a medal and some that win more than 100 in a single event.

After all, why is there such a huge gap between winning and losing countries? How much does it really take to become an Olympian? What comes into factor that separates an Olympian athlete to a non-Olympic level athlete? Being born in a specific country or continen have a direct impact on a person aiming to compete at the highest level?

This application was made trying to find trends to determine what it really takes to be an Olympian Country Champion.

GDP x Medals

The top 10 medal earning countries in the 2016 Summer Olympics (Rio de Janeiro) were: USA, Great Britain, China, Russia, Germany, Japan, France, South Korea, Italy and Australia. All of them were in the top 14 economies of the world for the same year.

The size of a nation's economy is commonly expressed as its Gross Domestic Product, or GDP, which measures the value of the output of all goods and services produced within the country in a year.

Women in the Olympics

1900

The first participation of women in the Olympics was in 1900. Twenty-two women (2.2%) out of a total of 997 athletes competed in five sports. The number of female athletes has been increasing since then.

GII

The Gender Inequality Index (GII) is an index for measurement of gender disparity. According to the UNDP, this index is a composite measure to quantify the loss of achievement within a country due to gender inequality. It uses three dimensions to measure opportunity cost: reproductive health, empowerment, and labor market participation.

It ranges from 0, where women and men fare equally, to 1, where one gender fares as poorly as possible in all measured dimensions.

Ratio of Women Athletes & GII

The ratio of women athletes was calculated by the number of women athletes divided by the total number of athletes for each country in 2016. It aimed to find a correlation between countries which have a high/low rate of women competitors and the gender equality/inequality.

Age

Age takes an immportant factor in an athlete's life. Age changes how our bodies use oxygen. With age, bones tend to shrink in size and density, weakening them and making them more susceptible to fracture. Muscles generally lose strength, endurance and flexibility. The way body burns calories (metabolism) slows down with age.
